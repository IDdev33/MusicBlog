Prior to me starting to learn how to program, I had a game on my laptop called "Skyrim". This game was released back in 2011 and still going on strong today thanks to the modding community. They're the only reason why it's still alive, more than a decade later. So when you install mods to it and once you have more than 100 mods installed, you're going to face problems such as random crashes, FPS drops, conflicts with meshes and textures, memory issues etc.

So then you're going to need to solve these problems right? Sometimes the solution is in the first search result of a google search. Sometimes the answer might be buried in a 6 year old forum thread on like page 124 of it and sometimes you may not find an answer at all, instead you're gonna have to dig deep and troubleshoot your current mod list. In order to do that you'll need to learn some tools. There was a tool for example called Tes5 edit that allows you to see the game records and it can show you what's conflicting with what. Very handy.

On some days, I'd spend probably hours trying to find the source of my problem and when I finally solve it, the satisfaction and feeling of accomplishment skyrockets. It feels extremely good and because of that, I've discovered that I actually do have the patience of sitting down and looking at a screen and testing a bunch of things.

I mention this because when I actually started programming, I faced tons and tons of issues and bugs and problems that I had to solve in all of my projects BUT I did not actually mind it. Yes it is frustrating when something does not work but we learn best through solving these problems.

Conclusion

Overall, I'm incredibly proud of myself for sticking through and pushing myself to learn new stuff all the time. You know when we first started the bootcamp, we were 6 people. The very next day we became 4. And after around 3 to 4 weeks we became just 2.
